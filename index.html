Does First Baron Control Predict Match Outcomes in Competitive League of Legends?

By Dat Nguyen — DSC 80, UCSD Spring 2026

Introduction

This project uses the 2022 Oracle's Elixir professional match dataset, the gold-standard source for competitive LoL statistics. The full dataset contains 150,348 rows and 165 columns, covering 12,529 unique games across 55 leagues. Our analysis focuses on the 21,282 complete team-level rows after cleaning.

Central Research Question: Do teams that secure the first Baron Nashor win at a significantly higher rate than teams that do not?

Baron Nashor is the most powerful neutral objective in the game. Securing it grants a strong team-wide buff and is widely considered the single most game-deciding moment in competitive play.

Data Cleaning and Exploratory Data Analysis

We applied four cleaning steps:

  1. Filter to team-level rows (position == 'team') — drops 125,290 player rows, keeps 25,058 team rows.
  2. Drop partial rows (datacompleteness == 'partial') — removes 3,776 rows from leagues that do not share full stats, leaving 21,282 complete rows.
  3. Convert boolean columns (firstbaron, result, etc.) from float to boolean dtype.
  4. Add gamelength_min — game length in minutes for readability.

Game lengths are roughly bell-shaped and centered around 31–32 minutes. Team kill counts are right-skewed, averaging around 14–15 kills per team.

First Baron is the strongest single objective predictor: teams that secure it win 84.9% of the time, far above First Tower (68%), First Blood (61%), First Herald (58%), and First Dragon (58%).

Winning teams hold a mean gold difference of +1,726 at 15 minutes vs. -1,726 for losing teams, though there is substantial overlap.

Assessment of Missingness

One column we believe is Not Missing At Random (NMAR) is split. Playoff games and international tournaments (Worlds, MSI) have no split assignment by design — the missingness is caused by the type of event, not a data collection failure. To make this column MAR, we would need an additional event_type column distinguishing regular season from playoff and international events.

Permutation Test 1 — split missingness vs. golddiffat15: Gold difference is structurally symmetric around zero in any complete-game subset. We fail to reject H0 (p > 0.05). The missingness of split does NOT depend on in-game gold performance.

Permutation Test 2 — split missingness vs. playoffs flag: We reject H0 (p < 0.0001). The playoffs column is significantly associated with split being missing, confirming our NMAR reasoning.

Hypothesis Testing

Test 1: Does securing first Baron lead to a higher win rate?

Null: The win rate of teams that secure first Baron is the same as teams that do not. Any difference is due to random chance.

Alternative: Teams that secure first Baron have a higher win rate (one-sided).

Test statistic: Difference in win rates (baron secured minus not secured). Significance level: 0.05.

Result: Observed difference = 0.663 (84.9% vs. 18.6%). p-value ≈ 0.0000. We reject the null. The data is consistent with teams that secure first Baron winning at a significantly higher rate — the effect is far too large to be explained by chance alone.

Test 2: Does Blue side have a higher win rate than Red side?

Null: Blue and Red side win at the same rate. Any difference is due to random chance.

Alternative: Blue and Red side win at different rates (two-sided).

Test statistic: Difference in win rates (Blue minus Red). Significance level: 0.05.

Result: Observed difference = 0.046 (52.3% vs. 47.7%). p-value ≈ 0.0000. We reject the null. The data is consistent with Blue side having a win rate advantage, aligning with the belief that Blue side's last-pick draft advantage provides a meaningful edge.

Framing a Prediction Problem

We frame this as a binary classification problem: predict whether a team wins (result = 1) or loses (result = 0) using only information available at the 15-minute mark.

Response variable: result — binary and perfectly balanced (50% win, 50% loss).

Evaluation metric: Accuracy. Since classes are perfectly balanced, accuracy is a fair and interpretable measure. A random baseline achieves 50%.

We intentionally exclude all post-game statistics (total kills, towers, barons) since those are only known after the game ends.

Baseline Model

A Logistic Regression model inside a single sklearn Pipeline with two features:

  • golddiffat15 (quantitative) — standardized with StandardScaler.
  • side (nominal) — one-hot encoded with OneHotEncoder.

Feature types: 1 quantitative, 0 ordinal, 1 nominal.

Test accuracy: 74.0% on the held-out test set. This is a reasonable starting point — well above the 50% random baseline — but a 26% error rate limits its practical usefulness. The linear decision boundary also cannot capture non-linear interactions between features.

Final Model

We switched to a Random Forest classifier and added three categories of improvements:

  • xpdiffat15 (quantitative) — XP advantage translates to higher champion levels, directly affecting fight outcomes.
  • csdiffat15 (quantitative) — CS difference measures farming efficiency, a separate dimension of early performance that gold alone misses.
  • early_obj_score (engineered) — sum of firstblood + firstdragon + firstherald (0–3), capturing overall early-game map control as a single numeric signal.

We tuned n_estimators, max_depth, and min_samples_leaf using GridSearchCV with 5-fold cross-validation.

Best params: max_depth=5, min_samples_leaf=1, n_estimators=200.

Test accuracy: 74.4% vs. baseline 74.0%. The improvement is modest but meaningful — the additional features give the model a richer view of the 15-minute game state, and Random Forest captures non-linear interactions that logistic regression cannot. A natural next step would be XGBoost, which typically outperforms Random Forest on tabular data through sequential error correction.

Fairness Analysis

Alternative: The model performs differently on LCK vs. LCS games.
